Frequently Asked Questions

Core vaccinations are crucial for protecting your dog from life-threatening diseases. These include:
  • Rabies: Required by law in most places, as it is fatal and transmissible to humans.
  • Distemper: A highly contagious viral disease affecting the respiratory, gastrointestinal, and nervous systems.
  • Parvovirus: A severe and often fatal disease causing vomiting, diarrhea, and dehydration, especially in puppies.
  • Adenovirus (Canine Hepatitis): Affects the liver and can be fatal.
Non-core vaccines (recommended based on lifestyle and location) include Bordetella (Kennel Cough), Leptospirosis, and Lyme Disease. Always consult your vet for the best vaccination schedule.

House training requires patience, consistency, and positive reinforcement. Follow these steps:
  1. Establish a Routine: Take your dog outside at regular intervals (e.g., after meals, naps, and playtime).
  2. Choose a Designated Spot: Always take your dog to the same area to encourage consistency.
  3. Use Positive Reinforcement: Praise and reward your dog with treats immediately after they eliminate outside.
  4. Watch for Signs: Sniffing, circling, or whining may indicate your dog needs to go.
  5. Clean Accidents Thoroughly: Use an enzymatic cleaner to remove odors and prevent repeat accidents.
Avoid punishment, as it can create fear and confusion. Most puppies are fully house-trained by 4-6 months.

Many human foods are dangerous for dogs and should be avoided. These include:
  • Chocolate: Contains theobromine, which is toxic and can cause vomiting, seizures, or death.
  • Grapes and Raisins: Can lead to kidney failure.
  • Onions and Garlic: Damage red blood cells, causing anemia.
  • Xylitol (found in sugar-free gum and candy): Causes a rapid drop in blood sugar and liver failure.
  • Alcohol: Even small amounts can be toxic.
  • Avocado: Contains persin, which can cause vomiting and diarrhea.
  • Caffeine: Found in coffee, tea, and energy drinks, it can be fatal in large amounts.
Always keep these foods out of reach and consult your vet if your dog ingests something toxic.

The amount of exercise a dog needs depends on their breed, age, and energy level:
  • High-Energy Breeds (e.g., Border Collies, Huskies): 1-2 hours of vigorous exercise daily.
  • Medium-Energy Breeds (e.g., Labradors, Golden Retrievers): 30-60 minutes of moderate exercise daily.
  • Low-Energy Breeds (e.g., Bulldogs, Basset Hounds): 20-30 minutes of light exercise daily.
Puppies and young dogs generally need more exercise, while senior dogs may require shorter, gentler activities. Mental stimulation (e.g., puzzle toys, training) is also important to keep your dog happy and healthy.

Introducing a new dog to your home requires careful planning to ensure a smooth transition:
  1. Prepare Your Home: Remove hazards and set up a designated space with a bed, food, water, and toys.
  2. Introduce Gradually: Allow your new dog to explore one room at a time.
  3. Introduce Family Members Slowly: Let the dog approach people at their own pace.
  4. Introduce Other Pets Carefully: Keep dogs on leashes during the first meeting and supervise interactions.
  5. Establish a Routine: Stick to a consistent schedule for feeding, walks, and playtime.
  6. Be Patient: Give your new dog time to adjust, which can take days or even weeks.
Provide plenty of positive reinforcement and avoid overwhelming your dog with too much attention or activity initially.
